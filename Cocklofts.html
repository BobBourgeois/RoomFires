5)Cockloft Fires etc   

Attacking a cockloftA cockloft is the void space between a flat roof and the joists of the top floor ceiling. The term is not used for truss construction. fire is about wetting the burning fuel surfaces and ventilating skillfully.

Gut reaction for many FFs is to “get ahead” of the fire and put water on the farthest reach of flame.  But knockdown is not about hitting flame.

A long flame across the underside of a roof deck can extend over a big area that is not pyrolyzingHeat-driven chemical reactions that produce fuel gases from solid & liquid fuels yet.  Applying water here can push the flame around, but it won't be an easy or effective way to kill the fire.

Knockdown is about wetting the surfaces that are making fuel gases.

Find those surfaces and paint them with water.  Start by knowing where the fire came from—how it got into the cockloft.  An electrical fire in the top floor ceiling is very different from a fire coming up the walls or chases; and both are very different from an exterior fire coming in through a soffit (porch fire etc).

Don't rely on just a TIC to track fire above the ceiling.  It takes time for the fire's heat to go through building materials (insulation, joists, plaster etc) to the ceiling or roof membrane where it's visible to the imager.  The delay can throw your decisions off.

If you do have a ceiling hole out ahead of the flame, you should be throwing water back toward the source of the fire.  The gold standard for cockloft attacks is a nozzle held above the joists.  The FF (keeping their head and body below the ceiling until the fire is controlled) directs the horizontal stream and sees the effect that it’s having.

Penetrating nozzles are also an option, as are combination nozzles using the sharp angle technique.  The key is to wet the surfaces that are actually burning and not just chase flame.

Try to restrict the fire’s air supply by limiting ceiling holes until you’re ready to put water where it's needed.  Wind and the building’s cockloft vents may work against you, so be realistic in your expectations.

A roof hole can have some combination of four objectives:  draw the fire in a good direction; turn a horizontal flame into a vertical flame; remove airborne fuel; improve conditions for the inside crews.  If you're sure that the cockloft is the only space that needs to be vented, let the interior crews keep control of the ceiling.  Don’t push it down.  They may want to limit the fire’s air supply; and they may be able to set up a helpful flow between a ceiling hole and the roof hole.

Check early for two ceilings and/or two roofs.

Etc

The success of a cockloft attack may also depend on putting out fire that is coming up from below (porches, wall voids, chases etc).  It may be helpful to think of the two battles as separate tasks and make them separate assignments.  Consider physically separating the jobs with an above-the-joists cockloft attack from another room.

When sizing up fire in walls and chases, don't forget about drop fire from melted tar and/or foamed plastic roofing products.

After the fight, get photos of the roof deck (from above and below) for the after-action review. Also take shots of the fire's path into the cockloft, and any building features that helped or hindered your efforts.

RQs

What effect does wind have?

In addition to changing the way the fire spreads, wind can change ventilation options, make size-up harder, and make sudden changes more likely.  It can create tough decisions.

Try to use the wind to your advantage but don't force a vent option that isn't really available.  Don't be distracted by dancing flames.  Stay focused on finding and wetting the burning fuel surfaces.  If the wind is having a big effect, nozzle-above-the-joists with an access hole at the upwind edge of the fire may be your best bet.

What is needed for a good nozzle-above-the-joists attack?

Good interior ladder capabilities and manageable kickback at the nozzle (i.e. sensible flow rate).

Nozzle held flat, completely above the joists, shooting parallel to the ceiling.

Information sharing among exterior eyes, top floor eyes, and roof eyes.

Coordinated actions.

What about difficult top-floor ceilings (wire lath, tin, two ceilings etc)?

Small-hole approaches (penetrating nozzles, sharp-angle etc) may work in some situations, but nozzle-above-the-joists is a more reliable solution—especially if you can find a ceiling area that doesn't have the wire lath etc.  Bathrooms and kitchens are good starting guesses.

Second ceilings were generally installed room-by-room, and any fire in the added voids can be put out room-by-room in a separate battle.  Be ready for collapse of the bottom ceiling.

What flow rates are needed for cockloft fires?

It depends on volume of fire and attack method chosen, but it's generally not a big water situation until the roof starts collapsing.  In the three-deckers that are common in the northeast, 50 GPM, hitting the right surfaces, is a lot of water.
