6)GPM and Safety Margins etc   

What gpm should be used for interior attack at common residential fires?

The more-is-better argument is popular in the U.S. right now, but any bandwagon should welcome scrutiny.  It’s okay to revisit any question.   

Is 150 gpm better than 100?  185 better than 150?  Would 200 be better still?

To see why all three answers are "no", break the issue into two queries.  What minimum do you need?  How much extra do you want as a margin of safety?  To tackle either question, we need to realize that:

  • There are multiple safety margins in play.
  • The margins don’t have the same minimums (reference points.)
  • Increases to a margin have diminishing value as the margin grows (i.e. increases have less value for margins that are already big.)
  • There are always tradeoffs.  Margin increases come at a price.

The main gpm safety margins are for knockdown and flow reduction.  You want to be okay if knocking the fire somehow requires more water than expected.  You want to be okay if a kink or some other mishap reduces your flow.

The knockdown margin's reference point is the minimum gpm that will black the fire out.  Any flow less than RP-Knockdown will fail to kill the fire.  Any flow more than RP-Knockdown will give you a margin of safety.  The actual number varies from fire to fire, so for planning purposes we take 30 gpm as an adequate residential RP-Knockdown.  It would easily cope with all but the largest residential rooms.

The flow-reduction reference point (RP-kink) is the lowest gpm that will provide a usable stream.  It’s the bottom of the nozzle’s operating range.

As with most safety margins, distance above the reference point will limit the benefit of any margin increase.  If your RP is 30, a flow increase from 40 to 60 will be more valuable than a boost from 140 to 160.  It’s unlikely that the higher numbers would ever come into play.

 

Figure 1 shows that, when some departments pushed residential target flows from 100 gpm to 150, they expanded knockdown margins that were already large, so the risk reduction was tiny.  Their kink margins shrank if they changed from adjustable-flow to fixed-flow nozzles (e.g. automatic to smooth-bore); grew if they kept adjustable-flow; and stayed the same if they kept fixed-flow.  So the only benefit of the flow increase went to departments that had adjustable flow nozzles and stayed with them. They saw a decline of their already low risk from kinks.


Comparing benefits to costs, we see several tradeoffs.  Two stand out.

  • Applying more water requires more exertion.
  • Applying more water requires more water.

More exertion should trigger questions about cardiac events, about using your air supply faster, reduced work time, and possibly exhaustion.  Using more water can of course cause you to run out of water, but the fear of running out of water can also have a ripple effect on other decisions and cost you time.

A disciplined approach to exertion efficiency and water efficiency can improve safety and effectiveness, but don't stop there.

Study more tradeoffs:  maneuverability, stream-reach, adaptivity, false sense of security, misdirection of training, water damage.  See the whole board.  Think beyond room knockdown—it's often the easy part. 

Remember that the tradeoffs are real and the bandwagon should spark skepticism:

    Be skeptical of recommendations that add difficulty or complexity to an operation.

    Be skeptical of the combat metaphor.  Talking about “meeting the fire with overwhelming force” is not a substitute for thinking about how to put the fire out.

    Be skeptical of anxiety-based language.  A nozzle team is unlikely to ever need or benefit from “every drop of water that you can get.”

    Be skeptical of comparisons to the past.  They’re often false.

    Be skeptical of training prop videos.  They’re selling something.

    Be skeptical of knee-jerk objectives.  “Apply as much water as possible” is a hollow objective that betrays a fundamental lack of understanding.

Etc

Safety margins are mostly invisible in case studies, even though nearly all bad results can be described as a margin going to zero.  It's hard to reconstruct how much leeway there was at any point during an incident, and even the task of identifying margins is probably foreign to most reviewers.

But there is value in the process.  Get in the habit of using the safety margin perspective as one view of every case.  Study the tradeoffs.  It’s a good way to learn about protective actions.

RQs

The RP of 30 gpm is a lot lower than some published calculations of required flow.  Why the discrepancy?

Published numbers have been creeping up for decades.  Anxiety played a role, but bad science and carelessness added to it.

Some authors assumed that nozzle teams extinguish fire by absorbing all the heat being released (HRR).  Not true.Fires are killed mostly by wetting the burning fuels—choking off the supply of pyrolysis gases.  Room Fires etc

Some authors assumed that todays fires require more water than those that were studied in the past.  Not true.See Room Fires etc

Some authors added safety margins to NFPA’s 100 gpm target flow.  Not needed since generous margins were already built in.

Field experience can be misleading when mental models are flawed.  Nozzle teams who fail because they can't wet the burning fuels sometimes misread the problemCockloft fires provide a common example.  See Flame Length Etc and Cockloft Fires etc as not enough water.

Does this safety margin analysis apply to cooling on approach (COA)?

Yes and no.  Trying to identify margins and tradeoffs is worthwhile but reference points are tough to pin down.  It's not just that the physics and chemistry are hard (most real world situations are complex and their variety is staggering.)  For each case, COA’s value as a protective measure also depends on which other protective measures are available.

The relevant threats are:

  • heat transfer to FFs
  • flame propagation through airborne fuel
  • flame spread over combustible surfaces
  • flame extension (i.e. flame becomes longer without propagating through additional fuel).

Each of these could have a margin in some fires but a lot of work needs to be done before we could know reference points—or even when the tactic will hurt or help.

When looking at tradeoffs don’t forget perception.  Once you open the nozzle you become visually impaired and hearing impaired.

And don’t forget to be skeptical.

Lots of jargon does not mean lots of understanding.

More water does not mean more effective.
