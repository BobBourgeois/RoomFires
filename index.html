1)Room Fires etc   

Knockdown of a well-involved room fire is mostly about wetting fuel surfaces.  Wetting the burning fuels quickly cuts the rates of heat release and fuel-gas production (pyrolysis).

It is not necessary to absorb the heat release rate (HRR) for the whole room—just for the surface area that is being hit by water at the moment.  If that area does not reignite immediately, the stream can make progress by wetting more fuel.  If most burning fuel surfaces can be painted with water, the knockdown happens in seconds.

Two progress-related concepts go a little deeper:

  • The first is progress instability—how quickly the fire could grow back.  If you imagine an interruption in water flow:  stable progress will persist after the water stops; unstable progress can quickly be lost (fire flashes back).
  • Water's progress will be stable on burning leaves and unstable on gasoline pool fires.  Well-involved room fires live in between.  Semi-stable progress persists for a short time as the stream moves on.
  • The other key concept is that, once you start making semi-stable progress against a room fire, you rapidly reduce the needed flow rate.

The initial GPM that will make semi-stable progress and darken down a room fire is situation-dependant, but it is the flow required to wet burning fuel surfaces.  Progress becomes more stable as more surface area becomes wet and stops pyrolizing.

Stopping pyrolysis of fuels is like stopping the flow of gas at a gas-fed fire.

Etc

These concepts also apply to fire in the voids of a building, but problems with detection and access often make it hard to wet all of the burning surfaces.

Fires on the outside of buildings (porches etc) tend to be fuel-controlled and have exposed fuel surfaces.  It is easy to make semi-stable and stable progress.  Modest flow rates achieve surprisingly good results.

Fires spreading through large areas, as in many commercial occupancies, may feature a race between the firefighters’ rate-of-progress and the fire’s rate-of-spread.  Higher flow rates may be required.

RQs

Why is there a minimum flow (GPM) required to make semi-stable progress in a typical room fire?

Some water will turn to steam before it reaches a fuel surface and some will evaporate from the surfaces.  You need each surface to stay wet for at least a few seconds.

Required GPM is related to volume of fire.  What is the link between volume of fire and progress stability?

Larger bodies of fire are better at continuing to transfer heat to fuel surfaces.  It takes a little more water to wet surfaces for the few seconds necessary.

How should people calculate the initial flow rates required to darken down room fires?

The Iowa formula (lwh/100) is based on testing and gives a reasonable estimate.  In practice, it’s very easy to deliver a lot more water than you need, so don’t overthink it.

Does the presence of plastics increase the gpm required to knock down a room fire?

No.

The fuels that might increase required gpm are those that affect progress stability.  Gasoline is an example.  Because gasoline’s flash point is much lower than the water's temperature, evaporation of its vapors cannot be stopped by the water.  Fire can flash back over an area already doused.

Even though parts of the room with no gasoline will darken down as usual, the overall knockdown might take a little more time and water.  (The difference isn’t always noticeable because common flow rates are so much higher than required minimums.)

Are “direct attack” and “indirect attack” useful terms?

The usefulness of these terms is limited by their varying definitions. I learned it as:

  • A “direct attack” kills the fire mostly by wetting fuel surfaces.  This includes water that hits the ceiling before it hits some other surface.
  • An “indirect attack” knocks the fire mostly by making steam.  As the phase change absorbs heat and the steam inerts the atmosphere, the flames are snuffed out.  Fuel surfaces then cool by transferring heat to the enviroment.  The production of fuel gases tapers off.

Using that definition, when is an indirect attack warranted?

In a few situations that combine:

  • Access difficulty
  • Enough confinement to hold the steam while the fuels cool
  • High levels of FF skill and coordination

(FFs should be ready for sudden changes during an indirect attack, since progress may be unstable.)

Put water on the stuff that's burning.  If the fire doesn't go out, it's probably because you didn't hit all the stuff.

A building fire isn't out until the fuels are cool and the atmosphere is vented.
