Room Fires etc

Knockdown and extinguishment of a well-involved room fire is mostly about wetting fuel surfaces. Wetting the burning fuels will quickly cut the rates of heat release and fuel-gas production (pyrolysis).

It is not necessary to absorb the heat release rate (HRR) for the entire room—just for the surface area that is being hit by water at the moment. If that area does not reignite immediately, the stream can make progress by wetting more fuel. If most fuel surfaces can be painted with water, the initial knockdown happens in seconds.

Two progress-related concepts help us understand the dynamics a little more deeply:

  • The first concept is progress instability—how quickly the fire could undo the headway being made. We can understand progress instability by imagining what will happen if the flow of water is suddenly interrupted. Stable extinguishment progress will persist when the water stops. Unstable progress can quickly be lost (fire flashes back) when the water stops.
  • Water will make stable progress on burning leaves and unstable progress on a gasoline pool fire. Well-involved room fires live in between. Progress can be semi-stable. Semi-stable progress will persist for a short time, allowing the stream to be redirected as needed.
  • The other key concept is that, once you begin making semi-stable progress against a room fire, the required flow rate decreases rapidly—oftentimes exponentially.

The initial GPM required to make semi-stable progress and darken down a room fire is situation-dependant but it is the flow required to wet fuel surfaces. Progress becomes more stable as more surface area becomes wet and stops pyrolizing. Stopping pyrolysis of fuels is like stopping the flow of gas at a gas-fed fire.

Etc

These concepts also apply to fire in the voids of a building, but problems with detection and access often make it hard to wet all of the burning surfaces.

Fires on the outside of buildings (porches etc) tend to be fuel-controlled and have exposed fuel surfaces. It is easy to make semi-stable and stable progress. Modest flow rates achieve surprisingly good results.

Fires spreading through large areas, as in many commercial occupancies, may feature a race between the firefighters’ rate-of-progress and the fire’s rate-of-spread. Higher flow rates may be required.

RQs

Why is there a minimum flow (gpm) required to make semi-stable progress in a typical room fire?

Some water will turn to steam before it reaches a fuel surface and some will evaporate from the surfaces. We need each surface to stay wet for at least a few seconds.

We know that required GPM is related to volume of fire. What is the link between volume of fire and progress stability?

Larger bodies of fire are better at continuing to transfer heat to fuel surfaces. It takes a little more water to wet surfaces for the few seconds necessary.

So how do we calculate the initial flow rates required to darken down room fires?

The Iowa formula (lwh/100) is based on testing and gives a reasonable estimate. In practice, it’s very easy to deliver a lot more water than you need, so don’t overthink it.

Does the presence of plastics increase the gpm required to knock down a room fire?

No.

The fuels that might increase required gpm are those that affect progress stability. Gasoline is an example. Because gasoline’s flash point is much lower than the water's temperature, evaporation of its vapors cannot be stopped by the water. Fire can flash back over an area already doused.

Even though parts of the room with no gasoline will darken down as usual, the overall knockdown time might be longer and a higher initial flow rate might be needed. (The difference isn’t always noticeable because common flow rates are so much higher than required minimums.)

Are “direct attack” and “indirect attack” useful terms?

The usefulness of these terms is limited by their varying definitions. I learned it as:

  • A “direct attack” knocks the fire down mostly by wetting fuel surfaces. This includes water that hits the ceiling before it hits some other surface.
  • An “indirect attack” knocks the fire mostly by making steam. As the phase change absorbs heat and the steam inerts the atmosphere, the flames are snuffed out. Fuel surfaces then cool by transferring heat to the enviroment, and the production of fuel gases tapers off.

Using that definition, when is an indirect attack warranted?

In a few situations that combine:

  • Access difficulty
  • Enough confinement to hold the steam while the fuels cool
  • High levels of FF skill and coordination

(Users should be ready for sudden changes during an indirect attack, since progress may be unstable.)
